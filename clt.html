3.2. Central Limit Theorem

Author

Kamil Filipek

3.2.1. The Core Idea

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is one of the most profound results in probability and statistics. It states:

If we draw a large number of independent random samples from any population with a finite mean μ and standard deviation σ, the distribution of sample means will be approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the original population distribution.

More precisely: for samples of size \(n\), the sample mean \(\bar{X}\) is approximately:

\[\bar{X} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mu,\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\]

The standard deviation of this sampling distribution is called the standard error:

\[SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]

3.2.2. Why It Matters

The CLT is the theoretical foundation for most frequentist hypothesis tests and confidence intervals. It explains why tools like the z-test and t-test produce valid results even when the underlying population is not normally distributed — as long as the sample is large enough (typically n ≥ 30 is used as a rough rule).

It also explains the law of large numbers: as sample size increases, the sample mean converges to the true population mean.

3.2.3. Demonstration in Python

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(0)

# Population: highly non-normal (uniform distribution between 2 and 6)
population = np.random.uniform(low=2, high=6, size=100_000)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(14, 4))

# Left: population distribution
axes[0].hist(population, bins=50, color="salmon", edgecolor="white")
axes[0].set_title("Population (uniform)")
axes[0].set_xlabel("Value")

# Middle and right: sampling distributions for different n
for ax, n, color in zip(axes[1:], [10, 100], ["steelblue", "seagreen"]):
    sample_means = [np.mean(np.random.choice(population, size=n, replace=True))
                    for _ in range(5_000)]
    ax.hist(sample_means, bins=50, color=color, edgecolor="white")
    ax.set_title(f"Sample means (n = {n})")
    ax.set_xlabel("Sample mean")
    ax.axvline(np.mean(population), color="red", linestyle="--", label=f"μ = {np.mean(population):.2f}")
    ax.legend()

plt.suptitle("Central Limit Theorem: Non-normal population → Normal sampling distribution")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Observation: Even though the population is perfectly flat (uniform), the distribution of sample means becomes bell-shaped for n = 10 and nearly perfectly normal for n = 100.

3.2.4. Standard Error and Sample Size

The standard error \(SE = \sigma / \sqrt{n}\) has a crucial implication: doubling the sample size halves the standard error (uncertainty about the mean). To halve SE again you need to quadruple n — diminishing returns at large sample sizes.

import numpy as np

sigma = 10  # population SD
ns = [10, 25, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1000]

print(f"{'n':>6}  {'SE':>8}")
for n in ns:
    se = sigma / np.sqrt(n)
    print(f"{n:>6}  {se:>8.3f}")

3.2.5. Confidence Intervals

The CLT lets us construct confidence intervals — ranges that contain the true population mean with a specified probability (e.g., 95%).

\[\bar{X} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

where \(z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96\) for a 95% CI.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(7)
sample = np.random.normal(loc=50, scale=12, size=80)   # 80 customer satisfaction scores

n    = len(sample)
mean = sample.mean()
se   = sample.std(ddof=1) / np.sqrt(n)
ci   = stats.t.interval(0.95, df=n-1, loc=mean, scale=se)

print(f"Sample mean:      {mean:.2f}")
print(f"Standard error:   {se:.3f}")
print(f"95% CI:           ({ci[0]:.2f}, {ci[1]:.2f})")

3.2.6. Practical Implications

Concept Business use
SE decreases with n Larger surveys give narrower confidence intervals — more precise estimates
CLT justifies z/t tests We can test hypotheses about means without knowing the population distribution
CI vs. p-value CIs communicate magnitude of uncertainty, not just significance — more informative for decision-making
Law of large numbers Averages over many transactions (e.g., insurance, casino) are highly predictable even when individual outcomes are not
Note

Bootstrap: a modern alternative

When n is small or the statistic is complex (median, correlation), the CLT may not apply. Bootstrapping resamples the data with replacement thousands of times to empirically construct the sampling distribution — no distributional assumptions required.
