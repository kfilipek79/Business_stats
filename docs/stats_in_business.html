1.1. Statistics in Business

Author

Kamil Filipek

1.1.1. Why Does Business Need Statistics?

Modern businesses generate data at an unprecedented scale — from transaction logs and customer behaviour to supply chain events and social media signals. Statistics provides the tools to extract actionable knowledge from this data. Without a statistical foundation, decisions are based on intuition alone, which is often biased and unreliable.

Key business functions that rely on statistics:

Function Statistical application
Marketing A/B testing, customer segmentation, churn prediction
Finance Risk modelling, portfolio optimisation, fraud detection
HR Performance evaluation, turnover analysis, pay equity
Operations Quality control, demand forecasting, process optimisation
Strategy Market research, scenario analysis, KPI benchmarking

1.1.2. Datafication of the World

Datafication refers to the transformation of social actions and everyday behaviours into quantifiable data. Virtually every human activity — shopping, commuting, communicating — now leaves a digital trace. This creates both opportunities (insights from behaviour patterns) and risks (privacy, algorithmic bias, surveillance).

Statisticians and data scientists are among the fastest-growing professions globally. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, employment in data-related occupations is projected to grow 35% between 2022 and 2032 — far above the average for all occupations.

1.1.3. Statistical Thinking vs. Statistical Manipulation

Statistics is a powerful tool, but it can be misused. Common sources of statistical manipulation:

  • Bad sampling — surveying only people who agree with you (self-selection bias)
  • Bad processing — cherry-picking time windows or subgroups that support a preferred conclusion
  • Bad interpretation — confusing correlation with causation, or ignoring effect size when p < 0.05
WarningThe p-value controversy

For decades, the threshold p < 0.05 was treated as a binary gate to “significance.” A landmark 2019 commentary in Nature signed by more than 800 scientists argued that this threshold should be retired in favour of reporting effect sizes, confidence intervals, and the full context of the finding (Black 2009).

1.1.4. Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning

Statistical reasoning is fundamentally inductive: we observe a sample and draw conclusions about a population. This is the opposite of deductive reasoning, which starts from general principles and derives specific conclusions.

  • Deduction: All humans are mortal → Socrates is human → Socrates is mortal.
  • Induction: We measured 200 customers, 68% preferred product A → probably most customers prefer product A.

Both approaches have their place, but empirical social science and business analytics are built on induction — and statistics gives it a rigorous formal framework.

1.1.5. The Statistics–AI Connection

Artificial intelligence, and machine learning in particular, is applied statistics at scale. Regression, Bayesian inference, matrix decomposition, and probabilistic graphical models are foundational to every major AI system. Understanding statistics is therefore not just useful for data analysis — it is essential for evaluating, deploying, and critically assessing AI tools in a business context.

Key statistical concepts used directly in AI/ML:

  • Probability distributions — the building blocks of generative models
  • Maximum likelihood estimation — the workhorse of model training
  • Bias–variance tradeoff — the central challenge in generalisation
  • Cross-validation — the standard approach to model evaluation
  • Regularisation — the statistical answer to overfitting

1.1.6. How to Learn Statistics Effectively

Statistics is best learned by doing. Passive reading is insufficient; you need to work through problems, implement methods in code, and critically examine real datasets.

Recommended learning path:

  1. Build intuition through worked examples and visualisations
  2. Derive key formulas manually at least once — understanding the mechanics prevents misuse
  3. Implement every method in Python or R
  4. Apply to a dataset from your own domain — the closer the data to your interests, the more motivated you will be
  5. Read critically — examine how statistics is used (and misused) in news, research papers, and business reports
