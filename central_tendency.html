2.1. Central Tendency

Author

Kamil Filipek

Descriptive statistics summarise a dataset using a small number of values that capture its essential characteristics. They fall into two groups: measures of central tendency (where the data is centred) and measures of variability (how spread out the data is).

2.1.1. Mean

The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of observations. It is the most commonly used measure of central tendency.

\[\bar{X} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i}{N}\]

data = [1, 4, 5, 6, 8, 3, 5]
mean = sum(data) / len(data)
print(f"Mean: {mean}")        # 4.57

import statistics
print(statistics.mean(data))  # 4.571428...

Weighted arithmetic mean is used when observations contribute unequally to the result (e.g., grades with different weights):

\[\bar{X}_w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i \cdot w_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i}\]

# Student grade example
grades  = [4, 3, 5]   # exam, activity, homework
weights = [5, 1, 3]   # weights

weighted_mean = sum(g * w for g, w in zip(grades, weights)) / sum(weights)
print(f"Final grade: {weighted_mean:.2f}")   # 4.22

When to use: Interval and ratio data, symmetric distributions without extreme outliers.

2.1.2. Median

The median is the middle value when observations are sorted. It divides the distribution into two equal halves.

  • Odd number of observations: median = the middle value
  • Even number of observations: median = average of the two middle values

\[M_{\text{odd}} = x_{\frac{n+1}{2}} \qquad M_{\text{even}} = \frac{x_{\frac{n}{2}} + x_{\frac{n}{2}+1}}{2}\]

import statistics

data_odd  = sorted([3, 2, 5, 18, 11, 6, 7, 9, 12])
data_even = sorted([3, 2, 5, 18, 11, 6, 7, 9, 12, 35])

print(statistics.median(data_odd))   # 7
print(statistics.median(data_even))  # 8.0  → (7+9)/2

When to use: Ordinal data, or interval/ratio data with skewed distributions or outliers (income, house prices, reaction times).

2.1.3. Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value. A dataset can be:

  • Unimodal — one mode
  • Bimodal / multimodal — two or more modes
  • No mode — all values occur equally often
import pandas as pd

data = [3, 2, 5, 18, 11, 6, 7, 9, 12, 35, 3, 5, 6, 2, 2, 3, 9, 5, 8, 8, 21, 3]

s = pd.Series(data)
print(s.mode())                 # 3 (most frequent value)
print(s.value_counts().head())  # full frequency table

When to use: Nominal and ordinal data; also useful for continuous data when binned into categories.

2.1.4. Choosing the Right Measure

Situation Best measure
Symmetric distribution, no extreme values Mean
Skewed distribution or outliers present Median
Nominal (categorical) data Mode
Reporting salary / income / house prices Median
Grading with different credit weights Weighted mean
NoteMean vs. Median in practice

Consider household income in a country. The mean is pulled up by a small number of very wealthy households, making it an overestimate of the “typical” income. The median is resistant to these extremes and better represents the middle of the distribution. This is why government reports on income typically report both.

2.1.5. Full Example in Python

import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Simulated monthly sales data (€ thousands)
sales = pd.Series([45, 52, 48, 61, 200, 55, 49, 53, 47, 50, 58, 44])

print(f"Mean:   {sales.mean():.1f}")
print(f"Median: {sales.median():.1f}")
print(f"Mode:   {sales.mode().values}")

# Visualise
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
sales.hist(bins=10, ax=ax, color="steelblue", edgecolor="white")
ax.axvline(sales.mean(),   color="red",    linestyle="--", label="Mean")
ax.axvline(sales.median(), color="orange", linestyle="-",  label="Median")
ax.set_xlabel("Monthly sales (€k)")
ax.set_title("Effect of an outlier on Mean vs. Median")
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The single outlier (€200k) raises the mean significantly while the median stays close to the cluster of typical months — a clear illustration of why median is preferred for skewed data.
